Understanding Complex Concussion: Beyond the Baseline

A look at the challenges of diagnosing and managing complex concussion cases—from prolonged recovery to co-occurring conditions—and what clinicians should watch for. Drawing on lessons from both military and civilian concussion care settings.

By Shawn Spooner, MD
Medical disclaimer: This article is for general education and is not personal medical advice. If you have urgent or worsening symptoms (severe headache, repeated vomiting, weakness, confusion, seizure, or rapidly deteriorating function), seek urgent evaluation.

Why “complex concussion” deserves its own conversation

Most concussions improve with time, reassurance, and a structured return to activity. But a meaningful subset of patients don’t follow the expected timeline. They may improve… then plateau. Or their symptoms scatter across multiple systems—headache, dizziness, sleep disruption, cognitive overload, mood changes, exercise intolerance—making the case feel “messy.”

That’s where complex concussion lives: not “worse” because the brain is permanently injured, but harder because multiple drivers can maintain symptoms, amplify distress, and cloud the clinical picture.

The mistake is treating that complexity like a moral failing (“they’re exaggerating”) or a single diagnosis (“it’s just post-concussion syndrome”). The better approach is to treat it like a systems problem with identifiable, treatable components.

Baseline is useful—until it isn’t

Baseline tests and symptom checklists can be helpful early, but complex cases often outgrow the baseline model. Why?

Symptoms aren’t specific. Headache, dizziness, fatigue, brain fog, insomnia, and anxiety overlap with migraine, cervical injury, vestibular disorders, depression/anxiety, PTSD, dysautonomia, and chronic pain.

Imaging is often normal. A normal CT or MRI is common and does not invalidate symptoms—concussion is primarily a functional injury early on, and later symptoms are frequently system-maintained rather than structural.

The “one injury, one symptom” story breaks down. Complex cases often involve layered contributors: neck + migraine + vestibular + sleep + stress response + deconditioning. Treating only one leaves the others to keep the fire lit.

A more useful framework: identify symptom drivers

Instead of asking, “Is this still the concussion?” a more actionable question is:

“What’s driving symptoms today—and what can we treat?”

Here are the most common drivers that show up in complex cases:

1) Headache phenotypes (often more than one)

Post-traumatic headache frequently evolves into recognizable patterns:

  • Migraine-type: light/sound sensitivity, nausea, throbbing, activity intolerance
  • Tension-type: pressure/tightness, stress-linked, muscle tension
  • Cervicogenic: neck/occipital start point, posture and neck movement sensitive
Clinical pearl: If you treat the headache driver well, “brain fog” and cognitive complaints often improve because attention, sleep, and stress physiology improve.

2) Cervical injury: the “silent partner”

Acceleration/deceleration mechanisms commonly strain the neck. Cervical injury can create:

  • headache
  • dizziness/unsteadiness
  • visual discomfort (especially with motion)
  • concentration problems via pain/sleep disruption

If you don’t assess the neck, you will miss a major contributor—and the patient may get mislabeled as having “persistent concussion.”

3) Vestibular and visual system dysfunction

This is one of the most fixable categories, and one of the most overlooked:

  • dizziness, motion sensitivity, nausea
  • difficulty in stores/crowds
  • reading fatigue and headaches
  • “I’m fine sitting still; I fall apart with movement”

Targeted vestibular and oculomotor therapy can be game-changing when appropriately prescribed.

4) Sleep disruption: symptom amplifier #1

Sleep affects pain thresholds, mood stability, attention, autonomic tone, and dizziness tolerance. Even mild insomnia can keep symptoms loud.

When sleep improves, the whole system often calms.

5) Autonomic intolerance and exertional symptoms

Some patients develop exercise intolerance, tachycardia, heat sensitivity, or orthostatic symptoms after concussion—sometimes overlapping with migraine biology, anxiety physiology, or deconditioning.

The answer is rarely “never exercise again.” It’s usually graded, structured exertion with monitoring and appropriate referrals when indicated.

6) Stress response and threat sensitivity

After injury—especially after frightening events—many patients develop heightened sensitivity to internal sensations and external triggers (light, noise, crowds). This is not “all in their head.” It’s a protective nervous system stuck on high alert.

Education, paced exposure, and the right rehabilitation plan reduce fear and improve tolerance.

7) Co-occurring mental health conditions and trauma

In military contexts, head injury may coexist with blast exposure, sleep deprivation, chronic stress, PTSD, and musculoskeletal pain. In civilian contexts, concussions frequently occur in settings involving fear, unpredictability, or high stakes (MVAs, assaults, work injuries).

In both settings, trauma physiology can amplify symptoms—and treating it is part of good concussion care, not an accusation.

What makes military and civilian cases feel different (but rhyme clinically)

The environments differ, but the clinical lessons overlap.

Military settings often involve operational demands, repeated exposures, polytrauma, sleep disruption, and psychological stressors. The biggest lesson is that recovery is rarely “one system at a time.” It’s integrated: sleep, pain, mood, vestibular, exertion, and function.

Civilian settings often involve work/school pressures, family obligations, fear of setbacks, and sometimes legal/occupational dynamics. The biggest lesson is that recovery is driven by function-based goals and symptom drivers—not by a perfect test result.

The common ground: the patient’s lived experience is real, the symptoms are often explainable, and the path forward is usually structured, not mysterious.

What patients and families should know

If you’re reading this as a patient (or supporting one):

  • You are not broken. Complex concussion doesn’t mean permanent damage.
  • You deserve a plan, not a label. “Persistent symptoms” should trigger a deeper assessment, not dismissal.
  • Progress is often non-linear. Plateaus happen. Flare-ups happen. The goal is an upward trend in tolerance and function over time.
  • The right rehabilitation is active, not passive. Prolonged avoidance tends to shrink your world. A good plan expands it carefully.

Clinician watch-for: practical checkpoints in complex cases

If you’re a clinician, here are high-yield “don’t miss” items:

Red flags (urgent evaluation as appropriate)

  • worsening severe headache, repeated vomiting
  • new focal neurologic deficits, seizure
  • rapidly worsening confusion/behavior change
  • concerning neck pain with neuro deficits
  • progressively declining function without explanation

The driver-based checklist

  • Headache phenotype(s): migraine vs tension vs cervicogenic; medication overuse; sleep/caffeine patterns
  • Cervical assessment: ROM, tenderness, provocation, proprioception, scapular/upper thoracic mechanics
  • Vestibular/oculomotor screen: VOR, smooth pursuit, saccades, convergence, motion sensitivity patterns
  • Sleep: insomnia, circadian disruption, OSA risk, nightmares/hyperarousal
  • Mood/trauma symptoms: anxiety, panic physiology, PTSD features, avoidance patterns
  • Autonomic/exertional tolerance: HR response, orthostasis, heat intolerance, graded exercise plan
  • Function: school/work tolerance, screen time, driving, shopping/crowds, exercise baseline

Documentation that holds up

Describe functional capacity (not just symptom scores): work/school hours tolerated, screen tolerance, exertion threshold, environmental triggers, and objective progression over time.

Frame recovery as treatable drivers and measurable goals (sleep, headache days, exertion minutes, vestibular triggers, work participation).

What “good care” looks like in complex concussion

Complex concussion care is rarely one magic intervention. It’s usually:

  1. Validation + education (reduces threat)
  2. Targeted rehab (neck, vestibular/vision, graded exertion)
  3. Headache management (phenotype-based)
  4. Sleep restoration
  5. Psychological support when indicated
  6. Consistent follow-up with measurable functional goals

In other words: structured, multidisciplinary, and optimistic—without being dismissive.
